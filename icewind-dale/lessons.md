# Lessons from Icewind Dale

**Campaign:** Rime of the Frostmaiden — Avarath Solvane (Eladrin Paladin, Oath of Vengeance)
**DM:** HeyDrewHayes
**Window:** April 2026 (S1) → 2026-05-16 (left after S11, at Paladin 2)

This file exists so future-Pax can skim it before joining a new D&D table and recognize the pattern earlier than 11 sessions.

---

## The one-line takeaway

> "Not bad enough to be obviously bad, just bad enough to slowly drain me."

That sentence is the whole lesson. The IWD campaign was *fine*. Nobody was a jerk. The table was warm. Drew was nice. And it was still draining me for 11 sessions because none of the things I actually wanted from a campaign were happening, and the absence of badness made the absence of goodness easy to ignore.

---

## What actually went wrong

The diagnosis from S9–S11 (confirmed across three consecutive sessions):

- **No prep.** Drew is improvisational by design. That works in moderation; it does not fill 3 hours when player momentum dies. S11 had 52% of runtime as player-improvised RP filling the prep vacuum, with the DM literally saying at 0:12 *"I had no way to prepare today."*
- **No combat challenge.** S10 basilisk: Krintas burned in Thors's chains before acting; DM said it was "supposed to be a lot harder." S11 bugbears: ended Round 1 on a Hold Person, party took zero damage.
- **No personal-arc movement.** Zero Avarath-specific beats across S9, S10, S11. The Auril / unnatural-winter / two-years-ago thread sat untouched.
- **No-combat sessions weren't even good for plot.** S9: 60 minutes shopping, empty dungeon, 0 plot reveals. Compare to Korvosa S6 (no combat, 4+ reveals + Blackjack + riot cliffhanger) or Myrrindar S6 (no combat, Wardstone Truth + Thalren intro + Rowan recruited). No-combat is a DM skill check, not an excuse.

The pattern: **lack of prep, not lack of effort or niceness.**

## Lessons for the next campaign

### 1. Set an explicit trial period when joining a new DM
Three sessions, then assess. Don't drift. The "wait and see" mindset is how 3 sessions becomes 11.

### 2. When something feels hollow, quantify the gap
Subjective dissatisfaction alone keeps you stuck ("am I being too picky?"). Subjective + numbers makes the call obvious. The metrics that worked:
- Minutes of designed-combat vs improv-filler vs random-encounter
- Plot reveals per session (0 vs 2–4)
- Personal-arc beats per PC per session
- % of runtime that was DM-prepped content vs player-improvised filling

The side-by-side comparison across campaigns (IWD S11 vs Myrrindar S7 vs Hellbreakers S4) was the unlock. Quantify the gap; don't just describe it.

### 3. Niceness is necessary but not sufficient
A welcoming, low-drama table is the baseline, not the win condition. "Everyone is so nice" is not a reason to stay if the actual game isn't delivering.

### 4. High StartPlaying reviews are not a craft signal in 5e
The review system rewards niceness over rigor. 5e's loose math doesn't punish DM error, Roll20's manual tracking hides it, and players who haven't played PF2e or other tightly-systemed games don't have a reference point. Don't take the rating at face value — read between the lines for prep, encounter design, and plot delivery.

### 5. Improv-only DMing has a structural ceiling
Drew is good at riffing, voices, and table vibes — and those things are real strengths. But they don't replace prep. If a DM tells you upfront they "run loose / improvise heavily," treat that as a yellow flag for any campaign longer than a short arc.

### 6. Session zero: get the awkward stuff in writing
- Stealth / invisibility rules (RAW vs house)
- Encounter difficulty calibration (do they use Moderate / High / Deadly budgets, or vibes?)
- Personal-arc cadence (do they design beats per PC, or are arcs player-driven?)
- Prep style (theater-of-the-mind, mapped encounters, statblock fidelity)

See also: [feedback_dnd_stealth_session_zero.md](../../.claude/projects/-home-nhanp-dnd-campaign-management/memory/feedback_dnd_stealth_session_zero.md).

### 7. The exit is the rep, not just the outcome
Identifying "not bad enough to be obviously bad, just bad enough to drain me" and *acting on it* is a transferable skill — it shows up in jobs, friendships, and other hobbies. The data-driven decision to leave was the practice, not just the result.

## What was actually good (for honesty)

Genuine strengths of Drew's table, preserved without varnish:
- **Voice work / NPC characterization** — every named NPC felt distinct, he committed to the bit
- **Roleplay-friendly** — gave players a lot of room to drive scenes
- **Welcoming table** — easy to show up to, no DM ego, no drama
- **Atmosphere** — when the cold of Icewind Dale landed, it landed

These are real. They just weren't enough to fill 3 hours twice a month when prep wasn't there.
